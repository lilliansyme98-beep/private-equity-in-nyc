Private equity has quietly reshaped New York City’s restaurant scene

By Lillian Syme

At least 216 restaurants across the city are backed by private equity firms. The brands look independent, but the investors are largely invisible.

Diljān is a bakery in Brooklyn Heights specializing in Afghan breads and pastries co-owned by Ali Zaman. It sits on a rare corridor where long-time local businesses and independent restaurants make up the majority of the neighborhood. It feels different from the neighborhood he grew up in, Astoria, where he said many of the local businesses are being bought out by corporations.

Zaman says he has been approached by outside investors more than ten times. He has turned most of them down. "It's kind of almost predatory," he said, "the way that some people function."

He is among a growing number of independent operators navigating a city where the restaurant landscape has shifted. Since 2020, a wave of private equity investment has quietly reshaped New York City's dining scene. Many of the brands that emerged from it still look and feel independent. The investors behind them are largely invisible.

Private equity firms have acquired at least 216 restaurant locations across New York City, with seven firms controlling 16 restaurant groups across the boroughs. Some of the deals involve recognizable brands like Joe & the Juice, which sold a majority stake to General Atlantic in a deal valued at roughly $641 million in 2023.

Others include Momofuku, Milk Bar and Bluestone Lane, which are backed by RSE Ventures, the investment firm founded by billionaire developer Stephen Ross.

But the single largest private equity presence in the city is one most New Yorkers have likely never heard of. TriSpan, a London based investment firm, holds ownership of major brands like Naya, Maman, and Rosa Mexicano totaling 58 locations.

Analysis shows that private equity investment is not just about ownership. It is driving rapid expansion across the city. TriSpan took Maman from one to 25 locations. It took Naya  from three locations to 29. Joe and the Juice went from 11 locations to 32 after investment by General Atlantic.

The concentration of private equity backed restaurants in Manhattan is not random. Data shows that the weighted average median household income in neighborhoods with the highest density of confirmed equity backed restaurants is $106,000, nearly double that of the outer borough average of $58,000.

Ten of the confirmed locations are located in transit hubs, including John F. Kennedy International Airport, LaGuardia, Penn Station and Hudson Yards. These findings illustrate that private equity backed restaurants are concentrated in both high income neighborhoods and high traffic locations.

Andrew Rigie, executive director of the New York City Hospitality Alliance, said rising rent, insurance costs and operating expenses have made it increasingly difficult for restaurants to open and survive without outside capital.

“If you don’t have an established reputation, a loyal following, and sufficient capital, there is very little room for error,” said Rigie.

A spike in demand following the end of lockdowns and extra cash from stimulus checks boosted the restaurant industry. When that inevitably fell off, who could afford to stay open changed. Landlords facing rising costs and higher interest rates favored operators that could offer stability, afford higher rents and show a track record of profitability.

“If you have three or four or five locations, landlords want you,” said Evan Franca, a Brooklyn restaurant owner and real-estate agent.

Zaman started his own hospitality group when he realized he wanted to expand. He was able to open his first business, Little Flower Cafe, a coffee shop in Astoria, using personal and family financing, but he says the economics of opening another business have become harder to maintain.

“If you want to open up a food business, at minimum it’s going to cost you $250,000,” he said.

“And that’s if you know what you’re doing.”

When looking to open Diljān, Zaman says he struggled to find a place with rent he could afford. He says owning a halal business is especially tough since he cannot rely on alcohol sales to offset high rents. He shopped around in buzzy areas like the West Village but couldn’t afford it.

“How can I possibly survive $23,000 a month?” he said.

Zaman said many of the deals offered too little money and required him to give up too much ownership.

“We were talking with some dude that was practically a billionaire,” Zaman said. “You hear it with the runaround for a year, and then it didn’t even give us like $50,000.”

Equity backed companies are turning to a different model, by recruiting experienced chefs to build new concepts designed to scale.

 

Somedays bakery is a croissant-focused bakery that was opened in early 2024. Focusing its menu on intricately shaped viennoiseries and buzzy flavors – it gained viral attention on social media soon after opening.

The project was led by Arlander Brown, a veteran baker, who was approached by Peter Phillips, founder and CEO of Chip City, a cookie chain backed by Enlightened Hospitality Investments, the growth equity firm co-founded by restaurateur Danny Meyer.

“The overall concept was to be a croissant based bakery where pretty much everything comes from one dough with the idea of expanding,” Brown said.

Somedays has expanded through a franchise model, allowing the company to grow without directly financing each new location. Brown said outside investment can help businesses grow, but often comes with tradeoffs.

“Anytime you either have a partner or you have investors, you’re going to lose a little bit of control,” he said.

Restaurant consultant Tommy Hunt has helped connect chefs in London looking to expand into New York City with investment groups who otherwise would struggle to open themselves. Recently, he’s helped open restaurants like Dean’s and Hawksmoor, both neighborhood restaurants focused around British cuisine.

Hunt says he tends to recommend the route of relying on wealthy individuals as investors rather than investment groups since the cost of opening and operating is incredibly high.

He says he would only go down the private equity route if he were focusing on expanding a concept that already proved to be successful. “PE is there for scale, growth and selling,” Hunt said.

As investor-backed brands expand their footprint across the city, business advocates and neighborhood groups say that local businesses have had a hard time keeping up with the changing costs to belong.

"The most vulnerable, legacy minority and women-owned and immigrant-owned businesses feel the strain most keenly,” said Francesca Bruce, Executive Director of the Grand Street Business Improvement District. The State Liquor Authority and city’s Small Business Services did not respond to multiple requests for comment.

The disparities show up in which types of restaurants receive outside investment.

Chinese restaurants are the second most common cuisine in New York City, with more than 2,200 locations registered with the city’s Department of Health. None appear among the private equity backed brands identified in this analysis. The same is true for Latin American, Caribbean and Korean cuisines, each among the 20 most common cuisines citywide.

Juice bars and smoothie shops, French restaurants and Australian cafe concepts appeared among private equity backed brands at rates five times higher than their share of the overall industry.

Several of the restaurants identified in the analysis still market themselves around local identity and founder driven stories. Bluestone Lane describes itself as “providing a genuine daily escape for all our locals,” while Maman says its cafes “reflect the unique character of the neighborhood they’re in.”

But the ownership behind many of the businesses was often difficult to trace. In many cases, ties to private equity firms were often not disclosed on restaurant websites or storefronts and could only be identified through investment announcements, liquor license records and corporate filings.

The New York Department of State confirmed that they do not collect the names of owners or principals of businesses registered in the state, making it impossible to identify who controls limited liability companies (LLCs) from public records alone.

For Zaman, that means that Astoria looks a lot different from the neighborhood he grew up in and opened his first cafe, as independent businesses are replaced by chains.

“You look at the streets, and now it’s all corporations,” Zaman said.

Methodology:

To identify private equity backed restaurants in New York City, this story cross referenced confirmed private equity deals from firm portfolio pages, press releases and trade publications against the NYC Department of Health restaurant inspection database, downloaded May 10, 2026. The database covers food establishments across the five boroughs regardless of alcohol service.

Deals were verified through primary sources, including private equity firm announcements and press releases distributed through PR Newswire and Business Wire. Deal amounts are included where publicly disclosed, though most transactions did not publicly report financial terms.

Restaurant groups were included only when a private equity investment could be confirmed through at least one primary source.

Four false positives were removed from the analysis after Russian restaurants with names ending in “naya” incorrectly matched search results for Naya Group.

The New York State Liquor Authority active license database was used as a secondary source to illustrate layered LLC ownership structures and was not part of the primary methodology because it includes only establishments with active liquor licenses.

Addresses used in the map were geocoded through the U.S. Census Bureau batch geocoder API. Eight addresses could not be geocoded and were excluded from the final map.
